Psychoanalytic Approach

Psychoanalysis is a way of understanding the mind, a method of treatment, and a stance toward human experience. It is not a set of techniques designed to produce specific outcomes, but a process of exploration that unfolds over time.

What Psychoanalysis Means in This Practice

Here, psychoanalysis involves meeting regularly—often multiple times per week—to speak freely about whatever comes to mind. This may seem simple, but it is a demanding process. It requires a willingness to stay with discomfort, to notice patterns, to explore thoughts and feelings that may be difficult to articulate.

The analyst's role is not to advise, interpret prematurely, or guide you toward a predetermined outcome. It is to listen—carefully, patiently, and with sustained attention to what you say and what remains unspoken. Over time, through this process, unconscious material begins to emerge. Dreams, slips of the tongue, repetitions in relationships, and the feelings that arise between you and the analyst all become part of the work.

What You Can Expect

Psychoanalytic work is structured around regularity and continuity. Sessions occur at consistent times, and maintaining this rhythm is essential. The work develops through sustained engagement—not in isolated moments of insight, but through the accumulation of repeated encounters with your own thoughts, feelings, and patterns of relating.

You can expect:

  • A contained, confidential space to speak freely
  • An analyst who listens without judgment
  • Attention to your unconscious processes—what you may not be aware of
  • Exploration of childhood experiences and their ongoing influence
  • Discussion of sexuality, relationships, symptoms, and desires
  • Work with transference—the feelings and expectations you bring to the therapeutic relationship
  • A slow, deepening process rather than quick symptom relief

What This Practice Does Not Offer

It is important to be clear about what this practice does not involve. This is not a place for:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) — no thought records, behavioral experiments, or cognitive restructuring
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) — no skills training or distress tolerance modules
  • Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT) — no disputing irrational beliefs
  • EMDR — no bilateral stimulation or reprocessing protocols
  • Hypnotherapy — no trance induction or suggestion
  • Psychological Testing — no standardized assessments or diagnostic evaluations
  • Solution-focused therapy — no focus on quick problem-solving
  • Coaching or self-help strategies — no action plans or prescriptive advice

If you are looking for any of the above approaches, they may be valuable, but they are not what is offered here.

Why Time and Continuity Matter

Psychoanalytic work cannot be condensed into a brief intervention. The unconscious does not reveal itself on demand, and meaningful change does not happen according to a schedule. This work requires time—not because it is inefficient, but because the process of becoming aware of unconscious patterns, working through conflicts, and developing new ways of relating is inherently gradual.

Continuity matters because psychoanalysis depends on the relationship that develops between analyst and analysand. It is through this relationship—its consistencies, disruptions, and evolving dynamics—that much of the work unfolds.

The Role of the Unconscious

Central to psychoanalysis is the idea that much of mental life operates outside conscious awareness. Your symptoms, difficulties, and patterns of behavior are not random—they are shaped by unconscious processes, by childhood experiences, by desires and conflicts that have not been fully thought through or put into words.

Psychoanalysis creates a space for these unconscious processes to become accessible. Through free association, attention to dreams, and exploration of transference, what has been outside awareness gradually comes into view. This does not eliminate suffering, but it can change your relationship to it—allowing for greater self-understanding and the possibility of living differently.

The Analyst's Stance

The psychoanalytic stance is one of curiosity, not certainty; listening, not advising; neutrality, not judgment.

The analyst does not position themselves as an expert who knows what is best for you. Instead, they listen for what you may not yet be able to hear in yourself. They attend to the places where you get stuck, the patterns that repeat, the feelings that arise in the room. They hold space for uncertainty, for contradiction, for the slow emergence of meaning.

On Change

Change in psychoanalysis is not about symptom elimination or achieving specific goals. It is structural—it involves a shift in how you relate to yourself and others, in what you are able to think about, in your capacity to tolerate complexity and ambiguity.

This kind of change is slow. It does not announce itself clearly. But it is deep, and it lasts.
