Building an Ethical Practice

This practice is built on a commitment to confidentiality, depth, and intellectual honesty. Psychoanalysis is not a set of techniques to be applied—it is a way of listening, a stance toward another person's suffering, and an ongoing inquiry into the unconscious dimensions of experience.

My work is grounded in the belief that symptoms, difficulties, and patterns of relating are not merely problems to be solved, but meaningful expressions of an individual's history, desires, and conflicts. The aim is not to eliminate what troubles you, but to understand it—to create a space where you can begin to hear yourself, notice what has been outside awareness, and explore what these experiences might mean.

A Psychoanalytic Method

The method here is psychoanalytic: regular sessions, continuity over time, attention to transference and countertransference, and a focus on the unconscious processes that shape your thoughts, feelings, and relationships. This is slow work. It does not promise quick outcomes or behavioral modification.

Instead, it offers the possibility of structural change—of coming to know yourself differently, of developing a more complex understanding of your inner world, and of finding new ways of being with yourself and others.

What This Practice Explores

The work here attends to the full range of human experience:

  • The unconscious—what operates beneath the surface of awareness
  • Childhood experiences and their ongoing influence
  • Sexuality and sexual life
  • Symptoms as communications rather than disorders
  • Patterns in relationships—past and present
  • Your relationship to yourself—how you think about who you are
  • Dreams, fantasies, and the imagination

A Non-Diagnostic Stance

This practice does not rely on rigid diagnostic categories or labeling. Instead of asking "what disorder do you have," the question is closer to "what is happening for you, and what might it mean?" Psychological formulations here are dynamic and evolving—they shift as the work progresses, as new material emerges, and as understanding deepens.

Commitment to Depth Over Speed

Psychoanalysis requires time. Change does not happen in a prescribed number of sessions or according to a treatment plan with measurable outcomes. It unfolds gradually, often in ways that are difficult to predict or quantify. The commitment here is to stay with complexity, to resist the pressure for quick solutions, and to allow the work to develop at its own pace.

A Note on Ethics and Confidentiality

Everything discussed in sessions is held in strict confidence. This practice is guided by ethical principles central to psychoanalytic work: respect for the individual, attention to the therapeutic relationship, and a commitment to creating a safe, contained space for exploration.

There is no judgment here—only curiosity, patience, and a willingness to listen.
